2. List 3 reasons why D3 was helpful and improved your visualization

D3 was helpful for my visualization because it made drawing parts of the graph simpler. If I were to not use D3, I would probably have to resort to drawing on the screen with a canvas element, which is much harder and more time consuming, since you draw on a canvas line by line. Another reason why D3 was helpful was that it allowed me to add style to my graphs directly without having to add style in my css file. Lastly, D3 was helpful because it was able to append the graph directly to the html and add more elements to it easily in order to show the graph on the screen.

3. List 3 reasons why D3 would not be the best tool for creating a visualization

D3 would not be the best tool for visualization if you have a simpler dataset that you want to display. D3 is complicated and sometimes difficult to figure out, so if you can save time using an alternative source to display a smaller dataset, then that would be best. D3 also may not be the best tool in the case of dealing with extremely large datasets. It could take a long time for D3 to process all of this data, so if you are looking for time efficient ways to display a large amount of information visually, then D3 might not be the best choice. Lastly, D3 is not the best choice if your data is in an unconventional format. If your data is not in the form of a csv, json, or other common data format that D3 can process, then it would be difficult to find any other work around besides reformatting your data. Instead of reformatting data, which could be time consuming, it might be better to just find an alternative way to visualize the data.

Accessible Data Visualization

4. Evaluate the accessibility of your dashboard based on the readings in the “Before you begin” section. What kinds of users might find this dashboard accessible and who might have more difficulty? What additional actions might you take to make this dashboard more accessible to all audiences?

People who do not have disabilities related to sight would easily find this dashboard accessible. However, those who are visually impaired may have a hard time seeing and interpreting the graphs on the screen. As stated in the article "A Comprehensive Guide to Accessible Data Visualization", there are people who are colorblind, have light sensitivity or other vision problems that affect how the dashboard would look to them. Therefore, one of the steps I took to mitigate these issues is to not have multiple colors on my graphs. All of the elements on my graph are one common color, and any outlines of shapes or text is black. This allows for colorblind people to still be able to read the graph. I also decided to use a darker instead of a lighter color, so that light sensitive people can still read the graph and not have a hard time seeing the bars against the white background. The article also mentioned making graphs and html elements screen reader friendly. An additional step I can take to make my dashboard more accessible is to add alt text to each html element, which tells a screen reader what an element on the screen represents.
